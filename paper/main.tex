\begin{abstract}
Open language models have outgrown the hardware most people own. We report an end-to-end case study of adapting and evaluating a compact model within a strict consumer budget. Beginning from Qwen3.5-2B, an approximately two-billion-parameter vision-language model, we fine-tune only its text transformer with 4-bit QLoRA on a single laptop GPU that has 4~GB of memory. Training peaks at 1.81~GB of VRAM, runs for 100 minutes, updates 0.58\% of the parameters, and produces a 42~MB adapter. We evaluate the merged model, which we call AVA-v2, across 17 benchmark configurations covering knowledge, reasoning, mathematics, code, multilingual arithmetic, and instruction following, and report a 95\% Wilson interval for every score. The capability profile is uneven: strong on science and multiple-choice knowledge (ARC-Challenge 82.0\%, ARC-Easy 92.0\%, MMLU 59.2\%) and weak on competition mathematics and strict instruction following. Two results are worth isolating. A 50-question GSM8K estimate of 48\% overstated the full 1{,}319-question result of 35.3\% by about thirteen points, which shows how small subsets can misrepresent compact models. Self-consistency added 8.7 points on GSM8K, whereas an in-model tool-use pathway activated on 0.6\% of problems and changed nothing. The adapter, merged weights, and a quantized GGUF ladder are publicly available.
\end{abstract}
