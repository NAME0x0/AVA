\section{Deployment}
\label{sec:deploy}

AVA-v2 is released in three forms: the 41.6~MiB QLoRA adapter, a merged half-precision checkpoint for use with standard transformers, and a ladder of GGUF quantizations for CPU and low-VRAM inference through llama.cpp \citep{gerganov2023llamacpp}, Ollama, and LM Studio. Table~\ref{tab:gguf} gives the size and measured perplexity of each quantization.

\begin{table*}[t]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrrl}
\toprule
Quantization & Size & RAM & Perplexity & vs.\ Q8\_0 \\
\midrule
IQ4\_XS & 1.11~GB & 1.6~GB & 2.5347 & $+2.0\%$ \\
Q4\_0 & 1.12~GB & 1.6~GB & 2.5244 & $+1.6\%$ \\
Q4\_K\_M & 1.19~GB & 1.7~GB & 2.4907 & $+0.25\%$ \\
Q5\_K\_M & 1.31~GB & 1.8~GB & --- & --- \\
Q8\_0 (reference) & 1.87~GB & 2.4~GB & 2.4844 & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{GGUF quantization ladder. Perplexity is measured on a held-out slice of the training distribution at 512-token context; lower is better. The sub-8-bit files use an importance matrix calibrated on that distribution. Q5\_K\_M was released without a perplexity measurement.}
\label{tab:gguf}
\end{table*}

The sub-8-bit files are built with an importance matrix calibrated on the model's own training distribution, which keeps their quality close to the 8-bit reference. The recommended Q4\_K\_M build is 1.19~GB and raises perplexity by 0.25\% over Q8\_0, while the smallest build fits in about 1.6~GB of memory. Every quantization runs without a GPU, so the model deploys on the same class of hardware that trained it. The evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:results} uses the Q8\_0 build, the most faithful of the ladder.
